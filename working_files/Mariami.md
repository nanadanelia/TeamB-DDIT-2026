#Part 1 Threat of New Entrants

--
Barriers to entry: Dense urban store networks in Tbilisi require years of capital to replicate. Mall anchor economics favor established tenants. Cold-chain logistics infrastructure is proprietary among top chains.

Sources of advantage for current leaders: Location density, supplier relationships, import distribution networks, and brand familiarity in neighborhood formats.

Likely new entrants: Hard-discount grocery formats (Lidl/Aldi equivalents) targeting underpenetrated regional cities. International fashion brands expanding via the new Tbilisi Outlet Village. E-commerce-native dark-store operators in Tbilisi.
Force rating: Medium


Strategic implication: Threat is real but segment-specific. Non-grocery is more exposed to international brand entrants; grocery is more exposed to discount-format entry in 
the regions. Lock in regional locations and premium mall space before challengers do.

-------
bargaining power of buyers

Who are the main buyers? - Individual Georgian households (primary) and tourists (secondary, concentrated in fashion, luxury, and electronics).

How concentrated are they?- Extremely fragmented — millions of individual consumers. Spending, however, is heavily concentrated in Tbilisi (81% of trade economy).

How easy is switching? - Very easy. Grocery chains compete store-to-store within walking distance. Cross-border platforms have expanded the comparison universe globally — Temu, Trendyol, and Farfetch are now realistic alternatives for non-perishables.

Force rating: Medium-High

Strategic implication: Loyalty programs are the underutilized defense. Most Georgian retailers compete on location and price alone, leaving switching costs near zero. Building first-party data and personalization capability is the primary lever

-----
Bargaining Power of Suppliers
Who are the critical suppliers? Domestic agricultural producers, international FMCG brands (Unilever, P&G, Nestlé, Coca-Cola), import distributors (Turkey and Russia as key origins), and premium fashion and electronics brand owners.

How concentrated are they? Domestic agriculture: fragmented and weak. FMCG brands: moderate. Premium fashion and electronics brand owners: high — they choose their Georgian partners, not the reverse.

How replaceable are they? Commodity inputs: highly replaceable. Branded FMCG: difficult, consumer demand is brand-specific. Premium international brands: irreplaceable in the near term.

Force rating: Low-Medium

Strategic implication: Grocery players should build private-label capability to reduce branded supplier dependence and protect margins. Non-grocery retailers should treat brand partnerships as strategic assets and invest in relationships that secure exclusive or preferential access.


-----
Threat of Substitutes
What alternatives solve the same job? Cross-border e-commerce platforms (Temu ~50% of cross-border transaction volumes, AliExpress, Amazon, Trendyol, Farfetch) for non-perishables across virtually every category. Wolt, Glovo, and Bolt for food and quick convenience. Traditional bazaars for fresh produce, especially outside Tbilisi. Local e-commerce platforms (Veli.Store, Extra.ge) for planned grocery without leaving home.

Why would customers switch? Price — Temu delivers non-perishables at 30–50% below local retail. Assortment — cross-border fashion platforms carry variety no Georgian mall replicates. Convenience — delivery aggregators eliminate the store trip entirely. Trust — bazaars are still preferred for fresh produce by a significant consumer segment.
Force rating: High
Strategic implication: Every non-perishable, non-urgent category is at structural risk of substitution. The defensible ground is fresh food, immediacy, and high-touch experience. Retailers without a credible delivery and digital presence will cede the broader consumer wallet to platforms they cannot outprice or out-assort.



-----
Competitive Rivalry
Number and type of competitors: Grocery is oligopolistic — Nikora (18.9%), Ori Nabiji (18.8%), Carrefour (13.6%), SPAR (12%), Magniti (9.2%) control ~72% of modern grocery. Non-grocery competition runs across mall venues (East Point, Galleria Tbilisi, Tbilisi Mall), high-street retail (Rustaveli Avenue), and a long tail of independent specialists.
Basis of competition: Price and location density in grocery. Brand exclusivity and experiential quality in non-grocery. Format differentiation — hypermarket vs. neighborhood vs. discount — is the emerging strategic divide.
Price intensity: High in grocery. Carrefour's entry established market-wide price transparency and promotional pressure that all chains must match. Lower in fashion and electronics where brand differentiation protects margin.
Innovation intensity: Low across the board. Loyalty technology, omnichannel integration, and supply chain digitalization lag regional peers significantly.
Force rating: High
Strategic implication: The mid-tier grocery segment (7–12% share) is structurally squeezed and faces consolidation within five years. In non-grocery, competitive advantage is shifting from physical presence to brand mix and omnichannel capability. New retail supply (Tbilisi Outlet Village) entering without proportional demand growth will intensify rivalry further

# Part 2. Value chain analysis

Map the chain.

| Stage | Main activities | Main actors | Profit concentration | Digital pressure points |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sourcing & Procurement |Supplier negotiations, import logistics, local farmer contracts  |Grocery chains (direct import), FMCG distributors, local agricultural cooperatives  | Low — fragmented supply, no private-label scale | ERP/supplier integration absent; no real-time demand signaling |
| Warehousing & Distribution | Storage, cold chain, regional DCs, fleet management |  In-house logistics (Nikora, Ori Nabiji); 3PL market nascent| Medium — top chains building this as a moat |  Cold chain gaps block regional expansion; route optimization underpenetrated|
| Store & Mall Operations |Merchandising, staffing, checkout, tenant mix management  | Nikora, Ori Nabiji, Carrefour, SPAR, Magniti; East Point, Galleria Tbilisi, Tbilisi Mall | High — primary profit pool across all formats |Self-checkout and app-based loyalty early-stage; tenant analytics absent  |
| Marketing & Loyalty | Promotions, loyalty programs, digital advertising, CRM | Chain marketing teams; aggregators (Wolt, Glovo) have more mature loyalty systems than most grocery chains | Medium — loyalty data is the undermonetized asset |Price comparison tools eroding margin; first-party data strategies largely absent  |

### Value chain interpretation
- Which stage appears most vulnerable? Last-Mile and Marketing. Aggregators have already inserted themselves between retailers and consumers. Cross-border platforms have removed Georgian retailers from the transaction entirely on non-perishables. Without loyalty data, retailers cannot personalize, cannot forecast demand accurately, and cannot defend margin against price-transparent substitutes.
- Which stage appears most defensible? Store Operations and Distribution. Physical networks and proprietary cold-chain logistics cannot be replicated digitally for perishable and urgent categories. This is the moat — valuable only if actively extended regionally before challengers identify the same opportunity.
- Where could a platform insert itself? At the intersection of Last-Mile and Marketing. A super-app or marketplace aggregating multiple retailers' inventory, owning the loyalty relationship, and orchestrating delivery would reduce Georgian retailers to fulfillment nodes. TBC Bank's digital ecosystem or a regional platform expanding from Turkey or Azerbaijan is the most plausible near-term vector.

# Part 3. Jobs-to-be-done competition

## Core customer job
What job is the customer trying to get done? Keep my household fed, clothed, equipped, and running — reliably, at a price that feels fair, without wasting more time and energy than necessary.

## Functional needs
List them. - Reliable availability of staples with no out-of-stocks. Prices proportionate to quality — not necessarily cheapest, but justified. Physical proximity for daily top-up missions. Fresh produce meeting Georgian culinary standards. Full-basket assortment completable in one trip or session. Fast checkout and fast delivery with minimal friction.

## Emotional or social needs
List them. - Feeling like a smart shopper — good value signals care for the family. Trust in food safety and authenticity, especially for meat, dairy, and imported goods. Social signaling through premium or mall retail for younger urban consumers. Community belonging through bazaar and neighborhood store interaction for older demographics. Identity expression through organic or specialty purchasing at Agrohub and importers.

## Alternative solutions
List both direct and indirect alternatives. - Direct: Competing grocery chains, competing malls and high-street boutiques, discount formats.
Indirect: Traditional bazaars for fresh produce and social ritual. Temu, AliExpress, Amazon, Trendyol, and Farfetch for non-perishables at structurally lower prices. Wolt, Glovo, and Bolt for the "I need this now without leaving home" job. Veli.Store and Extra.ge for planned grocery shopping online.
Latent: Subscription grocery and meal-kit services — not yet present at scale in Georgia, but demographic preconditions (rising urban incomes, time-pressed households, growing digital comfort) are approaching readiness.

## Interpretation
What does this reveal about hidden competition?

Georgian retail is not primarily losing to other Georgian retailers — it is losing to Temu on price, Wolt on time, and bazaars on trust. These are structurally different competitors requiring structurally different responses. Fresh food and immediate experience are the only jobs cross-border platforms genuinely cannot steal — this is the physical moat every retailer should be reinforcing, not neglecting. The bazaar will not be converted: outside Tbilisi it serves social and trust needs that modern formats don't replicate, and strategies premised on converting bazaar shoppers will systematically underdeliver. The biggest unserved job is eliminating planning altogether — the first mover who builds a subscription or meal-kit model captures a sticky loyalty position that price competition cannot erode.
