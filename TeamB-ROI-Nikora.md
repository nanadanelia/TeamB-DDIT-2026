# ROI Primer Worksheet

## Purpose

This worksheet builds the analytical skeleton of the transformation business case for Nikora’s online shopping service. It does not yet present a full financial model. Instead, it sets out the logic, value drivers, cost drivers, assumptions, and scenarios that will guide the financial modelling in Weekend 2.

## Part 1. Strategic Rationale

### Strategic rationale (two sentences)

How should **Nikora** respond to the rapid growth of digital commerce and rising consumer demand for convenience in order to **protect grocery market share and create a new digital revenue channel** before online retail penetration in Georgia increases significantly over the next decade?

If Nikora delays building an online shopping service, it risks losing digitally active urban customers to emerging digital competitors and alternative convenience channels while remaining dependent on a purely physical retail model.

### Connection to disruption diagnostic

Our disruption diagnostic identified several structural changes affecting Georgian retail: the growth of digital payments, increasing consumer familiarity with online shopping, and the rising importance of convenience and delivery-based services. These trends are gradually shifting consumer expectations toward omnichannel retail models where customers expect both physical stores and digital ordering options.

### Connection to competitive landscape

Our competitive landscape analysis showed that Georgian grocery competition is highly concentrated around **store proximity and dense convenience networks**. Retailers such as Magniti and Daily compete primarily through extremely dense store coverage that enables quick and frequent purchases.

Nikora does not operate the same “store on every corner” model, which limits its ability to compete purely on proximity. As a result, developing an **online ordering and delivery capability** represents a strategic alternative way to compete on convenience and accessibility.

### Why Nikora was chosen for this transformation case

Nikora was selected as the focal company for this transformation scenario for two strategic reasons.

First, Nikora is one of the largest organized grocery retailers in Georgia but currently **does not offer a fully developed online grocery ordering service**, creating a clear opportunity for digital channel expansion.

Second, because Nikora does not rely on an ultra-dense convenience store network like some competitors, a **digital grocery platform can help offset this structural disadvantage by providing convenience through technology rather than physical proximity**.

For these reasons, Nikora represents a strong case for analyzing how a traditional supermarket chain can use digital transformation to strengthen its competitive position in the evolving Georgian retail market.

## Part 2. Value Drivers

### 2.1 Revenue Growth

- Mechanism:  
Because of the launch of **Nikora Online** (app and website ordering with home delivery and click-and-collect), customers who currently shop only in-store gain a new ordering channel, which increases purchase frequency, captures convenience-driven demand, and prevents spending leakage to substitute channels.

- Scope of impact (which customer segment, product, or channel):  
Urban households in Tbilisi, Batumi, and Kutaisi; working professionals; families; repeat grocery shoppers; higher-frequency convenience purchases; digitally active customers.

- Order of magnitude estimate (do not model precisely yet):  
Online ordering could generate an additional **2% to 5% of total sales in the base case within 3 years**, with stronger adoption in dense urban areas.

- Data needed to quantify:  
  - Number of active Nikora customers in key cities  
  - Average basket size in-store vs. online  
  - Expected online order frequency per month  
  - Share of customers likely to adopt delivery or click-and-collect  
  - Cannibalization rate from existing store sales

- Evidence collected so far:  
  - Georgian retail is becoming more digital  
  - Non-cash transactions and e-commerce activity are rising  
  - Consumers increasingly value convenience and channel flexibility  
  - Grocery remains predominantly offline today, which creates an opportunity for channel expansion rather than immediate full substitution

### 2.2 Cost Reduction

- Mechanism:  
Because online ordering produces centralized digital demand data, Nikora can improve **inventory planning, basket forecasting, and promotion targeting**, which reduces stockouts, lowers waste in selected categories, and improves labor productivity in replenishment and merchandising.

- Scope of impact (which process, function, or cost line):  
Inventory management, store picking operations, promotional efficiency, selected labor processes, waste in perishables.

- Order of magnitude estimate:  
Potential **1% to 3% improvement in inventory-related efficiency** and **modest labor productivity gains** in pilot stores, depending on rollout design.

- Data needed to quantify:  
  - Current waste levels by category  
  - Current stockout rates  
  - Inventory turnover by store format  
  - Labor hours spent on replenishment and order preparation  
  - Promo redemption and conversion data

- Evidence collected so far:  
  International grocery retailers use digital demand data to improve forecasting, assortment decisions, and promotion effectiveness.

### 2.3 Risk Reduction

- Risk being mitigated:  
Loss of urban, digitally active customers to substitute channels and future digital-first grocery competitors.

- Mechanism:  
By creating its own online ordering service, Nikora reduces the risk that customers shift their grocery habits toward alternative platforms once digital grocery convenience becomes expected.

- Scope of impact:  
Urban customer retention, premium convenience segment, younger digital users, multi-channel households.

- Order of magnitude estimate:  
Could protect **3% to 7% of at-risk customer spending** in the medium term by retaining customers within the Nikora ecosystem.

- Data needed to quantify:  
  - Customer churn risk by demographic segment  
  - Survey data on online grocery interest  
  - Share of customers already using delivery apps for food or retail  
  - Competitive benchmark data from similar markets

- Evidence collected so far:  
  Consumer behavior in retail is shifting toward convenience, digital access, and cross-channel shopping. Once habits change, it becomes harder and more expensive to win those customers back.

### 2.4 Strategic Optionality

- Future opportunity enabled:  
Nikora could later build:
  - a loyalty app with personalized promotions  
  - subscription delivery or membership benefits  
  - retail media / supplier-funded placement  
  - data-driven pricing and assortment decisions  
  - dark-store or micro-fulfillment expansion in dense areas

- Why this capability is not accessible today:  
Without a first-party digital ordering platform, Nikora has limited direct digital customer interaction and limited transaction-level online behavioral data.

- Mechanism:  
The online service creates a digital customer relationship and data layer, which enables future monetization, personalization, and ecosystem expansion.

- Order of magnitude estimate (option value):  
Not yet precisely estimated, but potentially significant through **margin improvement, higher retention, and new supplier-funded revenue streams**.

- Evidence collected so far:  
In mature grocery markets, digital channels often become the foundation for loyalty, CRM, and retail media monetization.

### Cost driver notes

- Most uncertain cost item:  
Technology integration with existing store operations, inventory visibility, and order management processes.

- Source for benchmark estimates:  
International grocery e-commerce rollout benchmarks, digital commerce implementation projects, and directional assumptions adapted to Georgian market scale.

- What would cause cost to exceed the high end of the range:  
  - Complex legacy integration  
  - Poor inventory visibility at store level  
  - Need for larger in-house delivery fleet  
  - Delays in rollout  
  - Low pilot performance requiring redesign

## Part 4. Key Assumptions

### Assumption 1 (most important)

- Statement:  
We assume that a meaningful share of Nikora’s urban customers will adopt online grocery ordering if Nikora offers a reliable, easy-to-use service with acceptable delivery times and fees.

- Basis:  
Urban Georgian consumers already show rising comfort with digital payments, e-commerce, and app-based convenience services, while grocery remains a high-frequency category with strong convenience upside.

- Impact if wrong by 20%:  
If adoption is 20% lower than expected, the ROI decreases materially because revenue ramp is slower, fixed platform costs are spread across fewer orders, and payback extends by roughly 1 to 2 years.

### Assumption 2

- Statement:  
We assume average online basket size will be higher than a typical convenience-store basket because customers will combine more items per order when shopping digitally.

- Basis:  
Online grocery in many markets tends to increase basket planning and consolidation, especially for household replenishment shopping.

- Impact if wrong by 20%:  
If basket size is 20% lower than expected, revenue per order drops, delivery economics weaken, and margin improvement becomes harder to achieve.

### Assumption 3

- Statement:  
We assume Nikora can execute delivery and/or click-and-collect operations with service levels strong enough to maintain trust and repeat usage.

- Basis:  
Nikora already has a physical store network that can support local fulfillment in dense urban areas if operations are designed carefully.

- Impact if wrong by 20%:  
If service reliability is materially worse than expected, repeat orders fall, customer acquisition costs rise, and the payback period extends substantially.

### Additional assumptions (optional)

- Assumption 4:  
Cannibalization of existing store sales will be limited because the online channel primarily captures incremental convenience demand and protects at-risk customers.

- Assumption 5:  
Pilot-city economics will be stronger than regional-city economics because order density and delivery efficiency will be higher.

## Part 5. Scenario Sketch

### 5.1 Base Case

- One-sentence description of the most likely outcome:  
Nikora launches online ordering in major urban areas, achieves moderate customer adoption, and builds a sustainable omnichannel grocery model with gradual revenue growth and selective efficiency gains.

- Key assumption value used (e.g., AI adoption at industry median):  
Online adoption among target urban customers reaches a moderate level; average basket size is above in-store convenience level; rollout remains focused on a few dense cities first.

- Rough payback period estimate:  
**4 to 5 years**

- Why this is the most defensible scenario:  
It assumes realistic adoption, a phased rollout, and moderate execution success rather than breakthrough growth.

### 5.2 Upside Case

- One-sentence description of the best realistic outcome:  
Nikora’s online service scales quickly in major cities, repeat usage becomes strong, and the company gains meaningful first-mover advantage in digital grocery among Georgian supermarket chains.

- Key assumption value used (e.g., AI adoption at top quartile):  
High urban adoption, strong repeat ordering, efficient click-and-collect / delivery economics, and effective cross-selling through digital promotions.

- Rough payback period estimate:  
**2.5 to 3.5 years**

- What has to go right for this scenario to materialise:  
  - Excellent app experience  
  - Reliable fulfillment and delivery  
  - Strong marketing launch  
  - Competitive pricing and delivery fees  
  - High repeat order rates  
  - Successful use of loyalty and promotions to drive retention

### 5.3 Downside Case

- One-sentence description of the outcome if the most important risk materialises:  
Nikora launches online ordering, but customer adoption is slower than expected, operational costs are high, and the service remains a niche channel rather than a scalable growth engine.

- Key assumption value used (e.g., AI adoption at bottom quartile; implementation delay of six months):  
Low adoption, weaker basket size, rollout delays, service reliability issues, and higher-than-expected fulfillment costs.

- Rough payback period estimate:  
**6 to 8 years**, or potentially beyond initial planning horizon

- Does the client survive this scenario? How?  
Yes. Nikora survives because its core physical retail business remains intact, and the digital investment can be slowed, redesigned, or limited to priority urban markets.

### 5.4 Key differentiating assumption

- The single assumption that most changes the outcome across scenarios:  
**Customer adoption and repeat usage of Nikora’s online grocery service in dense urban markets**

- Why this assumption is the most uncertain:  
Because Georgia’s grocery market is still primarily offline, so actual willingness to switch part of grocery shopping online is uncertain even if overall digital commerce is growing.

- What research would reduce this uncertainty:  
  - Customer surveys on online grocery willingness  
  - Pilot testing in selected districts  
  - Benchmarking from similar emerging retail markets  
  - Focus groups on delivery fee tolerance, trust, and expected service levels
